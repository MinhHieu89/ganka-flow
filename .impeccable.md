## Design Context

### Users
Clinic employees at Ganka28 — a boutique ophthalmology clinic in Hanoi. Roles include doctors, technicians, nurses, cashiers, optical staff, and a manager. They use desktop monitors and laptops (design for both). Tablet support needed for exam room use. Users are clinic staff with varying tech proficiency — the interface must be immediately learnable with minimal training.

### Brand Personality
**Professional. Efficient. Precise.**

The interface should feel like a well-run clinical environment: clean, organized, confident. Not cold or institutional — but clearly medical-grade. Think "private specialty clinic" not "government hospital." Every element should justify its presence; nothing decorative without function.

Emotional goals: **confidence** (staff trust the system), **calm** (no visual noise during high-focus clinical work), **efficiency** (minimal clicks, clear information hierarchy).

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Minimal, clinical, spacious. Clean whites with purposeful color accents.
- **Color strategy**: Teal/blue-green primary (medical trust, calm precision). Neutral grays for structure. Semantic colors only for status and alerts — amber (waiting), emerald (success), red (destructive/urgent), sky (in-progress). Avoid decorative color.
- **References**: Untitled UI — specifically the generous whitespace, clean sidebar, and restrained use of color. Modern SaaS health platforms (Jane App, Cliniko) for workflow patterns.
- **Anti-references**: Government health systems (cluttered, dated, overwhelming). Dense enterprise EHR like Epic/Cerner. Generic purple SaaS dashboards.
- **Typography**: Inter Variable — clean, medical-appropriate. Strong weight hierarchy (bold headings, medium labels, regular body). No serif fonts.
- **Iconography**: Hugeicons with consistent stroke width. Functional, not decorative.
- **Theme**: Light mode primary, dark mode supported. Both must maintain clinical clarity.
- **Layout**: Sidebar navigation (collapsible), generous padding, clear section boundaries. Data tables should breathe — no cramped rows.

### Design Principles

1. **Clarity over cleverness** — Every screen serves a clinical workflow. Labels are explicit (Vietnamese with proper diacritics). No ambiguous icons without text. Information hierarchy guides the eye from most to least critical.

2. **Calm density** — Medical apps need to show a lot of data without overwhelming. Use whitespace strategically to group related information. Cards and sections create visual rhythm. Dense when needed (exam data), spacious when possible (dashboards).

3. **Status at a glance** — Color-coded status indicators, KPI cards, and queue counts let staff assess clinic state in seconds. Semantic colors are sacred — never use status colors decoratively.

4. **Speed of interaction** — Staff are mid-workflow with patients present. Minimize clicks, support keyboard navigation, use smart defaults. Forms should feel fast, not bureaucratic.

5. **Medical-grade trust** — The UI should feel as precise as the clinical work it supports. Aligned grids, consistent spacing, no visual bugs. If something looks broken, staff won't trust the data.
